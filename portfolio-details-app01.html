Going to the beach should be an escape from our busy lives. Unfortunately our beaches today are filled with trash and litter. Not only does this create an unwelcoming place but it has a huge impact on our coastal environment. Let’s look at the different ways our coastal environment is affected by our beach litter.

The Challenge

Develop a third-party mobile app that encourages local coastal communities to take action and help preserve their coastal environments.

Upshore Ideations
  • The beach isn’t just a great place to spend your Saturday afternoon at, the beach is home to countless creatures. As it may be easy to forget that visiting the beach is just like visiting someone’s home, belongings and waste is often left to pollute the area.
  • Upshore is here to control that damage. This app allows you to learn about your beach, how to properly dispose of waste, and connect with your community. Whether you want to navigate nearby trash or keep up with the latest coastal news, Upshore’s got you!

    • The ocean

    Hazardous chemicals from the litter can pollute soil and water. And through the food chain, these toxins not only make their way to animals but they make their way back to humans. Through litter, nitrogen and phosphorus can become abundant in water. This causes harmful algal blooms to accumulate quickly in waters and can block sunlight which ultimately creates a depletion of oxygen. The algae also can release toxins that can be harmful to both animals and humans.


    CORAL REEFS

    Trash such as plastic bags, singular used products, kids toys, produce containers, water bottles, etc. That makes its way into the sea and can snag on corals and block the sunlight needed for photosynthesis, or entangle and kill reef organisms and break or damage corals. The micro plastics were eaten by coral and other sea animals which can block their digestive tracts and potentially introduce toxics.


    ANIMALS

    Trash pollution at beaches affects more than 800 species. Over 100,000 seabirds, sea turtles, seals, and other marine mammals are killed every year by consuming the litter or being stuck in it. It’s easy for animals to mistake our trash for food so when they try to eat it, they choke or suffer internal injuries. Again the trash eventually ends up in our stomachs as plastic moves through the food chain. Animals like sea turtles also have trouble reproducing as climate change has affected the temperatures of the sand.


    THE ECONOMY

    When the public takes responsibility and disposes of their trash, cleaning waste isn’t too difficult. But when there is an excess amount of trash, then extra funds are needed to hire people to clean up littered areas. It costs the economy more money to clean up compared to if citizens were to properly dispose of waste themselves.


    PUBLIC HEALTH

    Not only is the economy at risk but so is the health of people. When people have contact with dirty water or sand it can be harmful. A 2013 study collected water samples from coastal regions and 10 percent of the samples failed to meet the EPA’s standards for safe swimming. About 3.5 million people get sick from water contaminated with sewage.
    Children are especially vulnerable as they tend to swallow a lot more water. They can consume criteria, fertilizers, feces, and trash. These effects can be as minor as a stomach ache to as severe as a neurological disorder. Those who have compromised immune systems are especially at risk. At times there will be a delay in systems so most people who experience illness from dirty waters don’t even know the cause of their symptoms.
